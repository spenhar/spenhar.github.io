DNA — deoxyribonucleic acid — has been used as a storage medium for digital information since at least 2012, when researchers at Harvard encoded a 5.27-megabit book into DNA strands.

The logic: DNA stores roughly 215 petabytes per gram. A hard drive stores roughly 1 terabyte per 100 grams. The entire world's data could theoretically fit in a shoebox.

The catch: read/write speeds are slow, error rates are non-trivial, and the process is expensive. But the density is unlike anything silicon has achieved, and DNA is stable for thousands of years under the right conditions.

This image sits at the intersection of storage, memory, and what we mean when we say something is *written*.
